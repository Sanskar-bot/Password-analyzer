What is a password?

A password is a secret string of characters used to verify that you are who you claim to be. When you create an account, the service stores a hash of your password — not the password itself. When you log in, the hash of what you typed is compared to the stored hash.

Authentication vs. Authorization

Authentication answers: "Are you who you say you are?" — passwords, biometrics, and security keys all solve this.
Authorization answers: "Are you allowed to do this?" — roles, permissions, and access controls solve this.

The most common password mistakes

1
Using personal information
Sanskar2004, Bruno123, Mom1990 — attackers who know you try these first. PassGuard's Attack Lab simulates exactly this.
2
Reusing passwords across sites
When one site is breached, credential stuffing attacks use your password on every other site you have an account at.
3
Predictable patterns
Password1! passes most "strength" requirements but is in every dictionary attack. Pattern detection matters more than complexity rules.
4
Too short
An 8-character password using all character classes has ~52 bits of entropy. Modern GPUs can brute-force this in hours. 16+ characters is the current recommendation.

What makes a password strong?

Strength is determined by how hard it is to guess — through brute force or pattern-based attacks. The key factors are length, entropy, character diversity, and resistance to dictionary/pattern matching.

Entropy: the math of unpredictability

Entropy (measured in bits) quantifies how hard a password is to guess. It depends on character set size and length:

Entropy = length × log₂(charset_size)
An 8-char password using lowercase only: 8 × log₂(26) = 8 × 4.7 = 37.6 bits
An 8-char password using all classes: 8 × log₂(95) = 8 × 6.57 = 52.5 bits
A 16-char lowercase password: 16 × 4.7 = 75 bits — stronger than the 8-char complex one.

Understanding how attacks work is the most effective way to build defenses. These are the main categories of password attacks.

Brute Force
Tries every possible combination. Slow but guaranteed to work. Modern GPUs can attempt billions of hashes per second.
Dictionary Attack
Uses precompiled wordlists of millions of common passwords and variations. Highly effective against predictable choices.
Credential Stuffing
Takes username/password pairs from one breach and tries them on other sites. Exploits password reuse — one breach becomes many.
Rainbow Tables
Precomputed hash-to-password lookup tables. Very fast for unsalted hashes. Defeated by salt in modern hash functions like bcrypt.
Password Spraying
Tries a small number of commonly used passwords against many accounts. Avoids lockout triggers by spreading attempts across targets.
Personalized Attack
Uses information specific to you — names, dates, pets — to generate a targeted dictionary. PassGuard simulates this in the Attack Lab.

What makes personalized attacks different

Standard dictionary attacks use wordlists of millions of common passwords. They find password123 and qwerty. But they miss passwords like Sanskar2004! — which passes every basic strength check.

A targeted attacker who knows you will construct a custom dictionary from:

Your name: Sanskar, Sandy, sanskar, SANSKAR
Your year: 2004, 04, 2004!, 04#
Combined: Sanskar2004, Sandy04, sanskar@2004, SANDY2004!
Pet name: Bruno123, Bruno2004, bruno!, BrunoSanskar
Partner: Priya+Sanskar, priya2004, Priya@123
A password like Bruno2004! scores 72/100 on standard strength but would be found at rank #83 in a personalized dictionary attack if your pet is Bruno and you were born in 2004.

Why use a password manager?

The human brain cannot reliably memorize dozens of strong, unique passwords. A password manager solves this by generating and storing unique passwords for every site, protected by one master password.

How they work

Your password vault is encrypted using your master password as the encryption key (via key derivation functions like Argon2 or PBKDF2). Even if the manager's servers are breached, your actual passwords remain encrypted.

What to look for

End-to-end encryption
Vault encrypted client-side. The server never sees your passwords.
Zero-knowledge architecture
The company cannot decrypt your vault even if compelled.
Breach monitoring
Alerts you when a site you use is compromised.
Strong master password + MFA
Your master password is the single point of failure. Make it a long passphrase and protect it with 2FA.

What is multi-factor authentication?

MFA requires two or more of the following to authenticate:

  • Something you know — password, PIN
  • Something you have — authenticator app, hardware key, phone
  • Something you are — fingerprint, face scan

Even if an attacker steals your password, MFA prevents them from logging in without your second factor.

MFA types ranked by security

Hardware Security Key
FIDO2/WebAuthn (YubiKey, etc.). Phishing-resistant. Cannot be intercepted remotely. Gold standard.
TOTP Authenticator App
Google Authenticator, Authy. Time-based one-time passwords. Strong, phishing-resistant if codes are not shared.
SMS One-Time Code
Better than nothing, but SIM-swapping and SS7 attacks can intercept SMS codes. Avoid if better options exist.
Magic Link (Email)
Secure as long as your email account is secure. Often not true MFA as it replaces the password entirely.

A security checklist based on NIST SP 800-63B, current threat models, and practical usability research.

1
Use unique passwords for every site
One breach should not cascade into dozens of compromised accounts. A password manager makes this achievable.
2
Prioritize length over complexity
A 20-character passphrase beats a 10-character "complex" password on every metric — entropy, memorability, and crack resistance.
3
Use a reputable password manager
Bitwarden, 1Password, and KeePass are open-audited options. Let the manager generate and remember passwords for you.
4
Enable MFA on every critical account
Email, banking, and cloud storage first. Use TOTP or hardware keys where possible, not SMS.
5
Store backup codes offline
When you enable MFA, download and print backup codes. Store them physically. Losing MFA access can permanently lock you out.
6
Avoid personal information in passwords
Names, birthdays, and pet names are the first things a targeted attacker tries. PassGuard's Attack Lab will show you exactly why.
7
Monitor for breaches
Use haveibeenpwned.com regularly. If your email appears in a breach, change the affected password immediately and check for reuse.
8
Change passwords after breaches, not on schedule
Mandatory periodic changes cause weaker passwords. Change only when there is a known reason to (breach, suspected compromise, shared device).
